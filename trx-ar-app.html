Exploring vehicles inside and out with augmented reality ↴
What could a digital manual do that an analog one can't?
Mercedes-Benz, BMW

We might not read owner's manuals anymore before driving a vehicle, but if we need to find some information quickly they should be easy to use.

The TRX App project started when FCA hosted an innovation night at their campus, with the winning team proposing to build a new way to explore features of this embargoed vehicle that was getting a lot of excitement from the community.

My colleagues and I designed and launched a CES app earlier in the year (start to finish in 8 weeks), so we were asked to do it again with this project.

The difference this time was that we had to use Augmented Reality.

v1.0

The palette for the app — Desert Cream and Midnight Dust — was inspired by this shot.

Since AR is the core feature of the app, let’s make it the home screen.

There aren't many AR apps on the market, so there are very few established conventions that people are familiar with. We had to take cues from a variety of common apps.

Given that AR was the primary focus, my recommendation was to make the live camera feed the focal point of the app.

A notable aspect of the three apps illustrated here for example, is that they get you to participate immediatly by giving you access to the main feature on the homescreen.

We decided to test this logic in v1.0. Not to mention that the overall design had to be straight-forward because of the deadline and limited experience with Unity (which we used build a map of detectable 3D objects, displayed below).

v2.0

After the first release, there's a number of things we were able to learn through testing and user feedback.

These informed our decisions for v2.0 which was going to have a much more structured release cycle, a long-term plan for feature developent, not to mention an evolved design language with greater flexibility.

The app also had to meet WCAG AA standards for accessibility, which is the reason we now have a landscape mode, among other things.

To make the design future-proof, I restructured the app architecture which now allows for easier access to a number of new features that are being added or will be added in subsequent releases.

"No navigation" means that every content area is within 1 step of the home page. This also allowed us to have floating UI elements that make the AR experience feel more immersive, in portrait but especially in landscape mode.
