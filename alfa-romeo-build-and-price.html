Alfa Romeo Build & Price configurator ↴
What does luxury mean, online? Below are some thoughts defining it for this project.
Speed

Onboarding speed

Bringing the user into the main application is done in 2 steps. The first is a cinematic vehicle selection page and the second is a grid view of the vehicle trims (models), thus increasing the difficulty and amount of options slowly, before getting to the main part of the configurator.

Loading speed

To achieve fast load times (and fast perceived load times), this application uses: lazy loading for images and sections of the page, as well as a re-organized dynamic data feed in order to show contextual options together (for example some Exterior options were part of Packages but were moved to the Exterior section).

Cognitive load

The way in which the application is laid out on desktop, tablet and mobile viewports makes it easy to orient yourself quickly. Within a single scroll, it's evident that the left side is dedicated to the product and the right to the feed of selectors.

Speed of completion

By using a single page design (while alternating layouts in each section) this application can provide exciting moments with highly engaging content and quiter moments with content that's made to be read or analysed. It's easy to find yourself at the bottom of the page having completed a build without a lot of effort.

Delight

Animation

Animation can often be overused, so it's used sparingly here, which maximizes its impact. For example, feed content fades-in, selectors animate when switching states, and notifications slide up from the bottom.

All this to get your attention at the right time or to provide a confirmation of your input.

Expensive but still informative

These vehicles and add-ons can be quite expensive, so detailed information is available and located conveniently. The 'Details' pill is de-prioritized visually, since it's not primary content, but when selected it opens a modal that displays all kinds of detailed information, and has easy cross-navigation for related selectors.

Focus

All the sections are on a single page, however they all look different in order keep things exciting, but also to allow peolpe to focus on a specific section.

Rewarding with beautiful images

While building the vehicle, users will likely want to compare options visually, so the images update with each selection. They can expand or rotate images where applicable and review the configuration in the editorial-styled Summary.

Ease of use

Scrolling as progress

Some people like to analyse each option while others want a quick preview of what they're buying. Scrolling through the page is inherently progressing in the build, so users have fast access to the end result.

In other words the application isn't standing in the way of them getting the luxury item they're exploring, it's enabling them in their research.

Contextual options

The dynamic feed of options was re-organized to display contextual options together, so that users don't have to scroll around looking for Exterior finishes outside of the Exterior section, for example.

Purposeful UI

Circles are for colours, pills are for details, mini tiles are for thumbnails, tiles are for packages, and rectangles are for buttons. Each function has a deditacted shape, purpose and behaviour.

Following you along

Some users might want to get a glimpse of their financial options, as they progress down the page. To accomodate for that, the 'Your Alfa Romeo' button opens a stylized drawer with that information. Additionally, every time a new selection is made, the price updates in the finances preview section, just left of the vehicle drawer.

No conflicts

A continuous experience

At the start of the project, one of the first things I did was to categorize conflicts. Aiming to either remove them or simplify them.

A "conflict" usually occurs when a new selection doesn't match the series of previous selections. This can be a result of business or engineering decisions, or that a feed hasn't been looked at for a while.

Build & Price configurators were plagued with these types of issues, displaying alerts almost every step of the way, creating such jarring experiences. Some are still like that.

Data structure

Users don't particularly care that one selection doesn't match with another in the back end. All they want is to configure a product and preview options.

If your configuration tool still displays warnings you are not doing enough to reduce friction, and you're confusing your users. Consider re-organizing your data feed and removing obstacles.

Meeting you halfway

For example, selecting a colour should display it no matter the price or package that it belongs to.

Selecting a bigger wheel size can automatically update the tire size, no need to say that they don't match, just update it.

In the event that you have a grouped set of selectors, you can display a non-intrusive, step-by-step flow.

Removing roadblocks

Additionally, the language used when communicating notifications should be easy to understand. Appropriately friendly language is understood faster than legal or engineering style of text.

Simplicity is hard to achieve, until you organize the complicated stuff.

Speed, delight, ease of use and lack of conflict are luxuries in real life, too.

When someone's buying an expensive product, they want to get it as quickly as possible because they're excited to start using it.

The excitement of seeing a new model or design and owning it, is something we all feel at one point or another.

You expect to be able to figure things out as you go along. You're not worried about these things, you feel confident it's fun to learn along the way.

When you interact with this new product you're allowed to do anything you try to do. There aren't any notifications mentioning "Settings" or telling you that you did something wrong. Or that in order to have this you must enable this.

It started with Alfa Romeo.
The rest of the brands followed suit shortly after.
